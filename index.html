Portfolio

I've done a thing or two in my day

Carleton's Ravens Racing

This is the 2017 car from the Carleton Raven's Racing Formula SAE team. During my co-op in Ottawa, I had the opportunity to work for the Formula SAE team. Often working 20+ hours a week on the car, I learned a lot about what it takes to get a mainly mechanical system performining to meet project objectives. The thumbnail photo is from the 2017 Ottawa-Gatineau Autoshow in March, where we unveiled the completed car for the first time this year. The photo above is from our rolling chassis test, which happened in early February. A lot of hard work went into meeting these deadlines, but it was well worth it.

Handy Dandy

Handy Dandy was a robotic arm designed by three of us in the Mechanical Engineering program. I designed the hand in SolidWorks, and then 3D printed it. I also lead the development of the software that controlled the entire project. Once complete, Handy Dandy was shown off at the 2016 UW March Break Open House, where it dazzled incoming students with its ability to perform gestures and pick up objects!

Raven's Racing: Powertrain

This is the KTM-450 which is the engine we run on the Carleton Formula SAE team. The KTM is a single cylinder 450cc engine which is run on motorcross bikes. During my time with the Carleton team, I specialized on the powertrain. This meant tuning and maintaining the engine, as well as taking care of our home-made dynamometer. Many long nights were spent on the dyno trying to get the engine working well. The picture above is a cool shot from one of our power runs. In this shot the exhaust header is glowing red hot as the exhaust gases inside reach a temperature of 900 degrees Celcius.

Go Go Myo Rocket!!

This was a project made during a hackathon hosted by our Engineering department. In our team of three we built a flight simulator in which you could fly around with the help of not one, but two Myo armbands! I worked with the in-app mechanics to try and ensure a realistic flight experience. While not a winner, I did learn a lot about about Unity, C#, and how painful the Myo's can be to work with.

Homemade Furnace

While quite cool, this is defintely the most dangerous project on here. A friend and I made this furnace with the intention of being able to melt aluminum. Using some Nickel-Chromium wire for heating, cinder blocks for protection, and a thermocouple to measure the temperature, we were able to get some good first results. While drawing ~10 Amps through the Nichrome wire we achieved temperatures of approximately 300 degrees Celcius. While high, they were not high enough to melt aluminum (~700 degrees).

VexU Prototype

During the summer of 2016 I was a part of the Ryerson Ram's Robotics team. While there I helped with the Mars Rover team, as well as the VexU team. On the rover team, I performed strength and weight calculations to make sure the arm could handle lifting objects, as well as take a fall if the rover were to tip due to the rough terrain. The photo above was the loading arm of one of the prototypes I helped build for the VexU team. When complete, this prototype was capable of throwing 'stars' nearly a meter high.

Hyperloop

The Hyperloop is a relatively new concept for transportation, outlined by Elon Musk in 2013. The ultimate goal for the Hyperloop is to achieve airplace line speeds on land - connecting the world like never before. Over the course of four months, with a team of about 30 active members, we were able to go from concept CAD to a functional protoype. This prototype was taken to Hawthorne, California in January of 2017 in order to compete in SpaceX's Hyperloop Pod competition. The above is of the Eddy Current Braking system, which I helped to design and manufacture.

Mars Rover Arm

This is a SolidWorks model of the Mars Rover arm I helped design while with the Ryerson Rams Robotics team. I performed some strength and weight calculations to make sure the arm could handle lifting objects, as well as taking a fall if the rover were to tip due to the rough martian terrain. I also did some research about the best materials to use for the construction of the arm.

Felix

Felix was an adorable platformer game built as a culminating assignment for my Gr.12 Computer Science course. The game was written from scratch using Java. It took me and my partner about four weeks to write. I got my hands dirty with a lot of stuff, including the in-game mechanics and level creation, among other things. Game development proved to be a fun but challenging medium. Through it however, I manged to learn a lot about what goes on under the hood of many 'simple' games developed today.
