Portfolio

I've done a thing or two in my day

Handy Dandy

Handy Dandy was a robotic arm designed by three of us in the Mechanical Engineering program. I designed the hand in SolidWorks, and then 3D printed it. I also lead the development of the software that controlled the entire project. Once complete, Handy Dandy was shown off at the 2016 UW March Break Open House, where it dazzled incoming students with it's ability to perform gestures and pick up objects!

VexU Prototype

This was the loading arm of one of the prototypes I helped build while a part of Ryerson Rams Robotics (R3). When complete, this prototype was capable of throwing 'stars' nearly a meter high.

Go Go Myo Rocket!!

This was a project made during a hackathon hosted by our Engineering department. In our team of three we built a flight simulator in which you could fly around with the help of not one, but two Myo armbands! While not a winner, it did teach us a lot about Unity, C#, and how painful the Myo's can be to work with.

Mars Rover Arm

This is a SolidWorks model of the Mars Rover arm I helped design while with the Ryerson Rams Robotics team. I performed some strength and weight calculations to make sure the arm could handle lifting objects, as well as taking a fall if the rover were to tip due to the rough martian terrain.

Felix

Felix was an adorable platformer game built as a culminating assignment for my Gr.12 Computer Science course. The game was written from scratch in Java in about four weeks by a team of two. Game development proved to be a fun but challenging medium. I learnt a lot about what goes on under the hood of many simple games developed today.

Homemade Furnace

While quite cool, this is defintely the most dangerous project on here. A friend and I made this furnace with the intention of being able to melt aluminum. Using some Nickel-Chromium wire for heating, cinder blocks for protection, and a thermocouple to measure the temperature, we were able to get some good first results. While drawing ~10 Amps through the Nichrome wire we achieved temperatures of approximately 300 degrees Celcius. While high, they were not high enough to melt aluminum (~700 degrees).
